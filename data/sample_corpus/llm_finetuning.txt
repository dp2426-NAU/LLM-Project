Fine-Tuning Large Language Models — Methods and Trade-offs

1. Full Fine-Tuning
Full fine-tuning updates all parameters of a pretrained model on a task-specific dataset.
While this achieves the best performance on in-distribution data, it is computationally
expensive (requiring the same VRAM as pretraining), risks catastrophic forgetting of
pretraining knowledge, and produces a separate model copy per task.

2. Instruction Fine-Tuning
Instruction tuning involves fine-tuning on (instruction, response) pairs to teach the model
to follow natural language directives. This is the basis for chat-oriented models like
InstructGPT, Claude, and Llama-2-Chat. The key insight is that a relatively small, high-quality
instruction dataset can dramatically improve alignment across a broad range of tasks.

3. Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning (PEFT)
PEFT methods update only a small fraction of model parameters while keeping most frozen.

LoRA (Low-Rank Adaptation): Decomposes weight updates into low-rank matrices:
W' = W + BA, where B ∈ R^(d×r) and A ∈ R^(r×k), with r << min(d,k)
Only A and B are trained, reducing parameters by >99% while matching full fine-tune quality.

Prefix Tuning: Prepends learnable continuous vectors (prefix tokens) to the input of each
transformer layer. The model parameters are frozen; only prefix embeddings are updated.

Adapter Layers: Inserts small trainable modules (bottleneck layers) between transformer
sublayers. Adapters add ~1-5% parameters and train ~60x faster than full fine-tuning.

4. Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF)
RLHF aligns model behavior with human preferences through three stages:
a) Supervised fine-tuning (SFT) on high-quality demonstrations
b) Reward model training on human preference comparisons
c) RL optimization (typically PPO) against the reward model with KL divergence penalty

The KL penalty prevents the policy from deviating too far from the SFT model, preserving
general capability while improving alignment.

5. Direct Preference Optimization (DPO)
DPO reformulates RLHF to avoid the RL training loop entirely. It derives the optimal policy
directly from preference data using a closed-form reparameterization, making training more
stable and computationally efficient than PPO-based RLHF.

6. Continual Learning Challenges
Fine-tuning on new domains risks catastrophic forgetting — degradation of performance on
original tasks. Common mitigations include: experience replay (mixing original data),
elastic weight consolidation (EWC), and sequential task training with task-specific heads.
